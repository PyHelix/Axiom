FINDING #1: abx_cycle_hgt_delay_resonance (PARTIAL — DELAY INVERSION AT SHORT CYCLES ONLY, 54 results, 13 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Delayed surveillance in adaptive antibiotic cycling interacts with
  HGT rate and compliance fatigue to create a resonance regime where multidrug
  resistance (MDR) burden exceeds both fixed cycling and zero-delay adaptive control.

  Method: Stochastic ODE model of bacterial population dynamics with susceptible (S),
  single-resistant (RA, RB), and dual-resistant (RAB) compartments. Horizontal gene
  transfer (HGT) enables resistance acquisition. Two strategies compared: fixed
  periodic drug cycling vs adaptive cycling that switches drugs based on delayed
  surveillance of resistance frequencies. Parameters varied: cycle period (3, 7, 14 days),
  surveillance delay (0, 2, 6 days), HGT rate (0.0015, 0.004), fatigue rate (0.02, 0.10).
  ~13,500 trials per workunit across 72 parameter combinations.

  Results:
  - At SHORT cycle periods (p=3 days): delay inversion OBSERVED. Zero-delay adaptive
    reduces resistance AUC by 0.29 vs fixed, but 6-day delay INCREASES it by +0.16,
    a swing of 0.45 AUC units. This is a genuine resonance — the adaptive controller
    overshoots when delay exceeds the cycle period.
  - At MEDIUM cycle periods (p=7 days): adaptive always better than fixed regardless
    of delay (delta -0.42 to -0.44 AUC). No inversion.
  - At LONG cycle periods (p=14 days): adaptive always better, delay-independent
    (delta -0.44 to -0.45 AUC). No inversion.
  - The experiment's own hypothesis signal (mean resonance excess = +0.01) is negligible
    because it only checks at p=7. The real effect is at p=3.
  - Time to 50% MDR is fast (~15 days) across all conditions with minimal strategy
    differentiation; the meaningful difference is in cumulative resistance burden.
  - Effect sizes are enormous (|d| > 60) because inter-run variance is tiny.
  - 54 results, 13 hosts — preliminary but highly consistent.

  Conclusion: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — Surveillance delay creates a resonance regime,
  but ONLY when the delay exceeds the cycling period. At 3-day cycles with 6-day
  delay, the adaptive controller receives information from >1 cycle ago, causing
  systematic drug-switching errors that amplify dual-resistant populations. This
  reveals a fundamental constraint on adaptive antibiotic cycling: the surveillance
  feedback loop must be faster than the switching frequency, or the controller
  destabilizes. At longer cycle periods (7+ days), even 6-day delays are within
  one cycle and adaptive control remains robustly beneficial. This has practical
  implications: rapid cycling protocols (popular in some ICU settings) require
  correspondingly rapid diagnostics, while slower cycling is more tolerant of
  surveillance lag.

FINDING #2: fisher_delay_adaptive_culling (NO EFFECT — INVASION SATURATES, 183 results, 24 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive spatial culling reduces invasion at low sensing delay but
  underperforms fixed culling at long delays when monitoring noise and control
  fatigue are present.

  Method: 1D Fisher-KPP reaction-diffusion model (n=220 sites, 420 simulated days)
  with periodic boundary conditions. An invasive species spreads from a central
  inoculum. Adaptive culling adjusts effort based on delayed, noisy estimates of
  invaded fraction. Parameters varied: delay (0, 7, 14 days), observation noise
  (0, 0.12), fatigue rate (0, 0.012), climate amplitude (0, 0.25). Fixed culling
  at constant rate 0.048; adaptive starts at 0.03 and adjusts. ~5,600 trials per
  workunit across 48 parameter combinations.

  Results:
  - Invaded fraction final = 1.000 for ALL conditions, both strategies, zero variance.
  - Front span max = 219 (full domain) for all conditions.
  - The ONLY detectable difference: adaptive reaches 20% invasion ~0.3 days faster
    than fixed on average (negligible given 420-day simulation).
  - Delta invaded fraction = 0.000 at all delays, noise levels, and fatigue rates.
  - No delay inversion observed (0/9 workunits with inversion flag).
  - 183 results from 24 hosts — sufficient data, conclusive null result.

  Conclusion: NO EFFECT — Both adaptive and fixed culling are overwhelmed by the
  invasion in this parameter regime. The culling rates (3-5% of population) are
  far below the growth rate (r=0.19), so the species reaches carrying capacity
  everywhere regardless of management strategy. The hypothesis cannot be tested
  in this regime because neither strategy achieves containment. This is a
  methodological ceiling effect: to test delay-dependent inversion, the experiment
  would need culling rates closer to the critical threshold where containment is
  marginal. As designed, the experiment is uninformative about the hypothesis.

NOTES
- Fisher adaptive culling experiment has a methodological issue: culling rates are
  too low relative to growth rate, causing universal saturation. A redesign with
  stronger culling or slower growth would be needed to test the delay-inversion
  hypothesis in a Fisher-KPP spatial context.
- ABX cycle finding is preliminary (N=54, only 4 results with hypothesis_signal data)
  but the delay-period interaction is highly consistent across all 54 workunits.
  More data needed especially at p=3.0 conditions.
- Several active experiments (HK consensus, chimera scaling, DLA drift, RFIM roughness,
  minority game, contact process, etc.) have no sanitized results yet.
