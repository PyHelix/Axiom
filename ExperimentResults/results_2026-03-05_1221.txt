FINDING #1: potts_pulse_anneal_resonance (CONFIRMED, 310 results, 22 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Periodic pulsed heating in a 3-state Potts model creates a resonance
  effect where specific pulse periods produce lower interface density (more ordered
  states) than random heating with the same duty cycle.

  Method: 44x44 Potts lattice, Metropolis dynamics with periodic hot pulses (beta_high
  at T=1.08*Tc) interspersed with cold annealing (T=0.78*Tc). Pulse periods swept
  from 4 to 64 sweeps. Compared periodic vs random (same duty cycle) final interface
  density across many trials per workunit.

  Results: Resonance index (peak periodic gain minus edge average) is positive in
  310/310 results, mean +0.0411 +/- 0.0051, Cohen's d = 8.10. Peak resonance occurs
  at period index ~2.15 (corresponding to period=12 sweeps). Periodic annealing beats
  random at periods 4, 12, 18, 40, 64 (100% of runs), but NOT at period=8 (only 35%
  positive). The non-monotonic gain profile confirms a true resonance, not a trivial
  ordering effect.

  Conclusion: SUPPORTED — Periodic thermal pulsing in the 3-state Potts model creates
  a genuine stochastic resonance in domain coarsening. The optimal pulse period (~12
  sweeps) likely corresponds to the characteristic domain wall relaxation time at the
  annealing temperature. The dip at period=8 reveals destructive interference between
  pulse timing and the natural coarsening dynamics, analogous to anti-resonance in
  driven oscillators. This demonstrates that synchronizing external perturbations to
  intrinsic relaxation timescales can accelerate ordering in multi-state systems.

FINDING #2: hypercycle_delay_dilution_rescue (PRELIMINARY, 169 results, 9 hosts)

  Hypothesis: In a hypercyclic replicator system with parasites, delayed adaptive
  dilution (using past parasite density to adjust dilution rate) outperforms immediate
  adaptive control by exploiting oscillatory dynamics.

  Method: 4-species hypercycle + parasite, continuous-time ODE with noise. Three
  strategies: fixed dilution, immediate adaptive, delayed adaptive (8 or 20 step delay).
  Measures parasite dominance fraction, species diversity, and collapse rate.

  Results: Delayed control improves diversity over immediate control in 100% of runs:
  delta_diversity = +0.000368 +/- 0.000064, d = 5.73. However, the dominance
  improvement is weak: delta_dominance = -0.000702 +/- 0.001236, d = -0.57 (58.6%
  better). The delayed advantage fraction (both diversity up AND dominance down) is
  25.1% of condition-pairs. Both adaptive strategies barely differ from fixed control:
  fixed_dom=0.121, now_dom=0.120, delayed_dom=0.120.

  Conclusion: WEAK SUPPORT — Delayed adaptive dilution produces a statistically
  significant but practically negligible diversity improvement (d=5.73 on a tiny effect).
  The core hypothesis of a delay-mediated rescue mechanism is not supported in a
  meaningful sense: all three control strategies produce nearly identical outcomes.
  The hypercycle-parasite system is robust to the control strategy, suggesting the
  catalytic network's intrinsic dynamics dominate over dilution effects.

FINDING #3: lyapunov_rmt_universality_gpu (REJECTED — Poisson, not GOE; 59 results, 6 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Nearest-neighbor spacing ratios of Lyapunov exponents for products of
  random matrices converge to GOE (Wigner surmise) universality across Gaussian,
  uniform, Bernoulli, and Student-t ensembles.

  Method: GPU-accelerated iterated QR decomposition for products of 50 random dxd
  matrices (d=200-2000). Compute Lyapunov exponents, then spacing ratio statistics.
  Compare mean spacing ratio to GOE (0.5307) vs Poisson (0.3863).

  Results: Mean spacing ratios across all ensembles: Gaussian=0.3899, Uniform=0.3901,
  Bernoulli=0.3901, Student-t=0.3901. All are close to Poisson (0.3863), far from
  GOE (0.5307). KS distances between ensembles are small (~0.060), confirming
  universality ACROSS ensembles. GOE fraction: 0.1% for all ensembles.

  Conclusion: REJECTED (GOE hypothesis) but CONFIRMED (universality) — The spacing
  distribution IS universal across matrix ensembles, but converges to Poisson statistics,
  not GOE. This is consistent with theoretical expectations: Lyapunov exponents of
  matrix products have uncorrelated (Poisson) level spacing because they arise from
  a multiplicative process, unlike eigenvalues of single random matrices which show
  GOE repulsion. The experiment confirms ensemble universality while correctly
  distinguishing it from spectral universality.

FINDING #4: hk_bounded_confidence_consensus (PRELIMINARY, 33 results, 5 hosts)

  Hypothesis: The Hegselmann-Krause bounded confidence model exhibits a critical
  confidence threshold epsilon_c with power-law divergence of consensus time.

  Method: N agents with opinions in [0,1], synchronous averaging within epsilon
  confidence radius. Sweeps N={100..2000} x epsilon={0.10..0.35}.

  Results: Critical epsilon decreases with system size: eps_c(100)=0.250,
  eps_c(200)=0.240, eps_c(500)=0.234, eps_c(1000)=0.219, eps_c(2000)=0.213.
  Extrapolated eps_c(inf) = 0.218. Consensus time at eps=0.22 scales as T ~ N^z
  with z = 0.984 (near-linear). At eps=0.20, cluster count ~1.9 for all N
  (consistently near the consensus/fragmentation boundary).

  Conclusion: SUPPORTED — The HK model shows a well-defined consensus-fragmentation
  transition near eps_c ~ 0.22 with consensus time growing nearly linearly with
  system size (z ~ 1.0). The near-linear dynamic exponent z ~ 1 suggests the
  consensus process is limited by sequential opinion cascade propagation across the
  population, rather than the parallel relaxation (z ~ 0.5) seen in mean-field
  systems. The finite-size shift of eps_c converges slowly, consistent with the
  model's lack of a sharp thermodynamic phase transition in the traditional sense.

FINDING #5: metapop_allee_climate_dispersal (CONFIRMED NEGATIVE, 251 results, 12 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Intermediate long-range dispersal fraction maximizes metapopulation
  persistence under climate-driven habitat shift with Allee effects.

  Method: 48-patch ring metapopulation with Allee threshold (0.22), climate-driven
  mortality shifting across the ring. Dispersal split between local (nearest-neighbor)
  and long-range (random patch) with fraction f in {0, 0.05, 0.15, 0.35}.

  Results: Persistence drops catastrophically with long-range fraction:
  f=0.00: 0.9991, f=0.05: 0.9985, f=0.15: 0.9834, f=0.35: 0.0381.
  No intermediate optimum (best is intermediate in only 8% of runs).
  Cohen's d for f=0 vs f=0.35: d = 5893.

  Conclusion: REJECTED — Long-range dispersal is catastrophic, not beneficial, for
  metapopulations with Allee effects under climate shift. The mechanism is clear:
  long-range dispersal dilutes emigrants across random patches, reducing local
  density below the Allee threshold and triggering population collapse. Local
  dispersal concentrates emigrants in adjacent habitat, maintaining viable densities
  as the climate envelope moves. This has direct implications for conservation:
  habitat corridors (enabling local movement) are far more effective than assisted
  migration (long-range translocation) for Allee-effect species tracking climate change.

FINDING #6: rfim_domain_wall_roughness (PRELIMINARY, 23 results, 6 hosts)

  Hypothesis: The domain wall roughness exponent zeta in the 2D RFIM varies
  continuously with disorder strength h, with a crossover from pure Ising to
  super-rough regime.

  Method: Zero-temperature Glauber dynamics on LxL lattice (L=16..128) with fixed
  +1/-1 boundary conditions forcing a domain wall. Roughness w(L) measured,
  zeta extracted from w ~ L^zeta.

  Results: Roughness exponent shows a clear non-monotonic peak:
  h=0.5: zeta=0.42+/-0.16, h=1.0: 0.67+/-0.07, h=1.5: 1.45+/-0.02 (PEAK),
  h=2.0: 1.13+/-0.01, h=2.5: 0.66+/-0.01, h=3.0: 0.37+/-0.01, h=3.5: 0.23+/-0.01,
  h=4.0: 0.16+/-0.02.

  Conclusion: SUPPORTED (non-monotonic) — The roughness exponent peaks sharply at
  h=1.5 with zeta~1.45, exceeding the theoretically predicted super-rough value of
  1.20. The peak likely corresponds to the disorder-driven phase transition where
  domain wall pinning is strongest. At weak disorder (h<1), thermal-like wandering
  dominates; at strong disorder (h>3), the domain wall fragments, reducing apparent
  roughness. The peak zeta=1.45 may reflect finite-size effects at L<=128.
