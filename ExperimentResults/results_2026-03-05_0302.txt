FINDING #1: tritrophic_delay_harvest_resilience (PRELIMINARY, 112 runs, 14 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive harvest control increases persistence and yield at low
  sensing delay/noise, but under high delay and seasonal forcing it overcorrects
  and raises collapse risk.

  Method: Tri-trophic food chain (resource-herbivore-predator) with PD-controller
  adaptive harvesting vs fixed harvesting. Tests 3 delay levels (0, 8, 20 steps),
  2 observation noise levels (0, 0.06), and 2 climate amplitudes (0, 0.25).
  Measures yield, persistence, and collapse fractions.

  Results:
  - Adaptive control INCREASES persistence by +22.8% +/- 2.6% vs fixed
    (Cohen's d = 8.65, N=1308 condition-pairs across 112 runs).
  - However, adaptive control DECREASES mean yield by -0.0055 +/- 0.0024
    (Cohen's d = -2.24, 0% of comparisons show positive yield delta).
  - Effect is consistent across all delay levels:
    delay=0:  persistence +23.0%, yield -0.0059
    delay=8:  persistence +23.0%, yield -0.0057
    delay=20: persistence +22.4%, yield -0.0049
  - No overharvest events detected in any condition.
  - Delay has minimal impact on the adaptive advantage — no degradation at high delay.

  Conclusion: PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — Adaptive harvest control strongly protects
  ecosystem persistence (the predator-herbivore-resource chain survives 23%
  more of the time), but at a consistent cost of ~5% lower yield. Contrary to the
  hypothesis, high feedback delay does NOT degrade adaptive performance —
  the persistence benefit remains robust across all delay levels. This suggests the
  PD controller's conservative response under delay is accidentally stabilizing:
  slower corrections prevent the overcorrection-collapse cycle that plagues
  aggressive harvesting. The yield-persistence tradeoff is a genuine management
  dilemma for resource systems where ecosystem collapse is irreversible.

FINDING #2: lorenz96_delay_assimilation_regime_shift (PRELIMINARY, 54 runs, 9 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive nudging reduces RMSE at short delay/low noise but can
  increase instability and extreme-event misestimation in high-delay/high-noise
  regimes, with stronger tradeoff at high forcing.

  Method: Lorenz-96 chaotic system (K=20 variables) with adaptive vs fixed
  gain data assimilation. Tests 3 delay levels (0, 6, 18 steps), 2 observation
  noise levels (0.2, 0.6), and 2 forcing regimes (F=8 weakly chaotic, F=12
  strongly chaotic). Measures RMSE, extreme-event fraction, and instability.

  Results:
  - At zero delay: adaptive nudging INCREASES RMSE by +0.633 +/- 0.253
    (100% of runs show higher RMSE — worse performance, d=2.50).
  - At delay=6: adaptive is neutral, delta RMSE = -0.0015 +/- 0.026
    (33% negative = slight improvement but within noise).
  - At delay=18: adaptive REDUCES RMSE by -0.011 +/- 0.018
    (70% of runs show improvement, d=-0.61).
  - No instability events in any condition. Extreme-event fractions are
    indistinguishable between methods.

  Conclusion: REJECTED (for the stated hypothesis) — The pattern is opposite
  to predicted. At zero delay, where adaptive control should perform best,
  it actually performs worst — the PD controller over-reacts to innovation
  signals, amplifying analysis error rather than correcting it. At high delay,
  the sluggish response accidentally acts as a low-pass filter on the chaotic
  innovations, producing a small but consistent RMSE improvement. This is a
  cautionary finding for operational data assimilation: responsive gain
  adaptation in chaotic systems can be counterproductive when the system's
  Lyapunov timescale is shorter than the control response time.

FINDING #3: schelling_delay_tax_reentrance (CONFIRMED, 184 runs, 11 hosts)

  Hypothesis: In a Schelling segregation model with distance-dependent
  relocation tax, adaptive tax control shows a reentrant advantage at
  intermediate feedback delay (d=4 or d=12 steps) vs extremes (d=0 or d=24).

  Method: 32x32 Schelling grid with two groups + vacancies. Adaptive policy
  adjusts relocation tax based on delayed segregation measurement. Compares
  composite segregation reduction across 4 delay levels (0, 4, 12, 24).
  "Reentrant index" = mean composite gain at mid delays minus mean at edge delays.

  Results:
  - Mean reentrant index = -0.00123 +/- 0.00102 (Cohen's d = -1.20).
  - Only 24/184 runs (13%) show positive reentrant index.
  - Segregation gain increases monotonically with delay:
    d=0: seg_gain = +0.00063
    d=4: seg_gain = +0.00087
    d=12: seg_gain = +0.00255
    d=24: seg_gain = +0.00432
  - Best delay is mid (4 or 12) in only 55% of runs.
  - Adaptive control provides a small positive composite gain (+0.0118 +/- 0.0012)
    but the gain is LARGEST at d=24, not at mid delays.

  Conclusion: REJECTED — No reentrant delay window exists in the Schelling tax
  model. The adaptive segregation-reduction benefit increases monotonically with
  delay. Higher delay means the tax responds to older (higher) segregation
  measurements, producing larger taxes that more effectively deter long-distance
  moves. Rather than delay degrading control, it acts as a memory of past
  segregation peaks, creating a persistently higher tax floor. This is a genuine
  negative result: not all delayed feedback systems exhibit non-monotonic
  performance windows.

FINDING #4: grayscott_delay_pulse_feedback (CONFIRMED, 144 runs, 15 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive pulsed feed improves Gray-Scott pattern persistence at
  low feedback delay but underperforms fixed pulses at high delay under sensor
  noise and heterogeneity.

  Method: 84x84 Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion with periodic pulsed feeding.
  Adaptive mode adjusts pulse amplitude based on delayed variance observations.
  Tests 3 delay levels (0, 8, 20), 2 sensor noise levels, 2 heterogeneity
  levels. Measures pattern persistence (fraction of time pattern score > threshold).

  Results:
  - Adaptive persistence gain at delay=0: -0.0326 +/- 0.0012 (Cohen's d = -27.1)
  - Adaptive persistence gain at delay=20: -0.0336 +/- 0.0027 (Cohen's d = -12.3)
  - 100% of runs show negative gain at both delay levels.
  - Delay inversion (low delay helps, high delay hurts) observed in 0% of runs.
  - Both delay conditions show nearly identical underperformance.

  Conclusion: REJECTED — Adaptive pulsed feeding consistently REDUCES pattern
  persistence relative to fixed pulses, regardless of delay. The Gray-Scott
  system's patterns are sensitive to feed-rate perturbations near the
  Turing-Hopf boundary; the adaptive controller's variance-targeting feedback
  pushes the system away from the stable pattern regime. The fixed-amplitude
  pulse, being tuned to the pattern-sustaining region, outperforms any
  variance-reactive strategy. No delay-dependent crossover exists.

FINDING #5: fisher_allee_movinghabitat_delay_culling_window (PRELIMINARY, 82 runs, 8 hosts)

  Hypothesis: In a Fisher-type reaction-diffusion with Allee threshold under
  a moving climate envelope, delayed adaptive culling shows a mid-delay
  reentrant advantage over both low and high delay.

  Method: 1D periodic domain (N=256) with Allee-logistic growth under a
  drifting Gaussian carrying-capacity peak. Adaptive culling adjusts intensity
  based on delayed invasion-front observations. Tests 3 delay levels (0, 6, 12
  days), 3 climate speeds, 2 Allee thresholds. Measures invaded fraction
  (adaptive - fixed).

  Results:
  - delta_invaded at d=0:  +0.02820 +/- 0.00010 (Cohen's d = 280.9)
  - delta_invaded at d=6:  +0.02814 +/- 0.00006 (Cohen's d = 490.9)
  - delta_invaded at d=12: +0.02863 +/- 0.00010 (Cohen's d = 272.5)
  - All delays show positive delta (adaptive has MORE invasion than fixed).
  - Reentrant mid-delay advantage observed in 0% of runs.
  - Effect sizes are enormous but the absolute magnitude is small (~2.8%).

  Conclusion: REJECTED — No reentrant delay window exists. Adaptive culling
  actually produces slightly MORE invaded area than fixed culling across all
  delay levels (by ~2.8 percentage points). The adaptive controller focuses
  culling effort at the invasion front based on edge-detection gradients, but
  this front-tracking strategy under a moving climate envelope allows diffuse
  spread behind the main front to escape control. The fixed uniform culling rate,
  while less efficient at the front, suppresses background regrowth better.
  The effect is delay-independent because the invasion dynamics are dominated
  by the climate-speed/Allee interaction, not by control timing.
