KEY SCIENTIFIC FINDINGS

FINDING #1: pit_delay_inhibitor_resonance (CONFIRMED REPLICATION, 66 runs, 43 seeds, 9 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Delayed adaptive inhibitor control creates a non-monotonic pit-depth resonance at intermediate flow pulse periods, amplified by heterogeneity.

  Method: Each trial simulates corrosion under fixed, pulse, and adaptive inhibitor policies across periods (24, 72, 216), delays (0, 10, 30), heterogeneity (0.05, 0.22), and chloride levels (0.7, 1.1), then summarizes resonance and policy contrasts.

  Results: resonance_mean = +0.06631 +/- 0.00428 (66/66 positive, Cohen's d=15.50, host-mean span=0.00882); adaptive_minus_fixed_mean = -0.003485 +/- 0.001690 (66/66 negative, d=-2.06, host-mean span=0.00351).

  Conclusion: SUPPORTED — delayed adaptive control continues to generate an intermediate-period resonance signature while still reducing mean pit depth versus fixed dosing. This implies a real control tradeoff: feedback delay can amplify frequency-selective corrosion dynamics even when average damage remains improved. Practically, controllers should optimize both mean suppression and resonance vulnerability rather than treating them as the same objective.

FINDING #2: chemostat_delay_crossfeeding_resonance (PRELIMINARY, 86 runs, 33 seeds, 15 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive nutrient control with sensing delay exhibits period-dependent resonance that can reduce coexistence under intermediate forcing periods.

  Method: Two-species cross-feeding chemostat simulations compared fixed/pulse/adaptive inflow control over forcing periods (18, 54, 162), delays (0, 8, 24), heterogeneity (0.04, 0.18), and shock amplitudes (0.5, 1.0).

  Results: adaptive_vs_pulse_coexistence = -0.004039 +/- 0.002330 (82/86 negative, d=-1.73, host-mean span=0.00735); resonance_strength = -0.000548 +/- 0.003493 (40 positive / 46 negative, d=-0.16, host-mean span=0.01203).

  Conclusion: INCONCLUSIVE (practically weak) — adaptive control is slightly worse than pulse on coexistence in this window, but the effect is below 0.5 percentage points and the resonance metric itself is sign-mixed. The data do not yet support a strong mechanistic resonance claim in this ecosystem model. Current evidence points to small performance differences rather than a robust delayed-control phase effect.

FINDING #3: interdep_flow_memory_shedding_tradeoff (PRELIMINARY, 42 runs, 24 seeds, 9 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Intermediate memory windows improve interdependent-network survival relative to edge windows under delayed risk forecasts.

  Method: Coupled-network cascade simulations sweep memory windows (1, 3, 8, 16) and forecast biases (-0.15, 0, +0.15), then compare medium-window vs edge-window survival performance.

  Results: mean_medium_window_gain = -0.000871 +/- 0.001106 (11 positive / 31 negative, d=-0.79, host-mean span=0.00313).

  Conclusion: REJECTED (preliminary) — medium memory windows are not outperforming edge windows in this sample and are slightly worse on average. This weakly favors monotone or edge-dominant behavior rather than a reentrant memory optimum. More seeds are still required to determine whether the small negative shift is structural or sampling noise.

FINDING #4: moran_delay_migration_window (PRELIMINARY, 56 runs, 20 seeds, 11 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Delayed adaptive migration feedback yields a reentrant delay window that improves persistence/absorption outcomes versus fixed migration.

  Method: Two-deme Moran-process simulations compare adaptive vs fixed migration at delays (0, 3, 9, 18), summarizing rescue and absorption-time gains plus a center-vs-edge window index.

  Results: center_delay_advantage = -36.57 +/- 16.93 (56/56 negative, d=-2.16, host-mean span=45.45); best_absorption_gain_mean = +1367.20 +/- 13.41 (56/56 positive, d=101.93); diversity_gain_mean_mean = +0.002056 +/- 0.001262 (56/56 positive, d=1.63).

  Conclusion: REJECTED (preliminary) — adaptive migration improves absorption outcomes overall, but the improvement concentrates at edge delays rather than intermediate delays. The intended reentrant center-delay mechanism is not observed in this batch. Scientifically, this suggests delay helps mainly through boundary regimes in this model, not through a central timing resonance.

FINDING #5: ssh_delay_dephasing_edge_window (INCONCLUSIVE, 22 runs, 8 seeds, 5 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Delayed adaptive dephasing should create a non-monotonic delay window that improves edge-to-edge transfer in a disordered SSH chain.

  Method: Disordered SSH transport simulations compare fixed vs adaptive dephasing at delays (0, 2, 6, 12) and summarize transfer, localization (IPR), and window signatures.

  Results: window_index_mean = +1.447e-06 +/- 7.879e-08 (22/22 positive, d=18.37); best_transfer_gain_mean = -1.737e-04 +/- 1.610e-06 (22/22 negative, d=-107.91); ipr_gain_mean = +3.588e-02 +/- 3.514e-04 (22/22 positive, d=102.10).

  Conclusion: INCONCLUSIVE — localization and window metrics shift in the expected direction, but best transfer gain is negative and independent seeds are <10. The present data do not yet establish that delayed adaptive dephasing improves transport performance. Additional seed coverage is required to separate true transport effects from structured finite-sample bias.

FINDING #6: nonmarkov_trap_release_window (INCONCLUSIVE, 5 runs, 4 seeds, 2 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Intermediate trap-memory windows produce positive reentrant transport gain relative to short/long windows.

  Method: Trap-dominated random-walk simulations sweep memory windows (1, 4, 12, 24) and score diffusion against trap/release costs, then compute center-vs-edge reentrant gain.

  Results: mean_reentrant_gain = +0.000155 +/- 0.000023 (5/5 positive, d=6.72, host-mean span=3.48e-05).

  Conclusion: INCONCLUSIVE — direction is positive but sample size is too small for a scientific claim (N<10 seeds). The effect may represent a true reentrant window, but this cycle alone cannot validate it. More independent seeds are required before interpretation.
