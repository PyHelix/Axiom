FINDING #1: chemostat_delay_crossfeeding_resonance (REJECTED, 416 runs, 16 hosts)

  Hypothesis: In a two-species cross-feeding chemostat under periodic inflow forcing,
  adaptive nutrient control with sensing delay exhibits a non-monotonic resonance window
  where intermediate forcing periods harm coexistence more than short or long periods.

  Method: Two bacterial species cross-feed in a chemostat with periodic nutrient inflow.
  Three control policies (fixed, pulse, adaptive) are tested across 3 forcing periods
  (18, 54, 162 steps), 3 delay levels (0, 8, 24), 2 heterogeneity levels, and 2 shock
  amplitudes. Each run completes ~1500+ internal trials. Coexistence is measured as the
  ratio of minority to majority species biomass in the simulation tail.

  Results:
  - Resonance strength: mean=-0.000399 +/- 0.003502, Cohen's d=-0.11 (186 pos / 230 neg)
  - Adaptive vs Fixed coexistence: mean=-0.003192 +/- 0.002348, d=-1.36 (33 pos / 383 neg)
  - Adaptive vs Pulse coexistence: mean=-0.003893 +/- 0.002300, d=-1.69 (19 pos / 397 neg)
  - Delay penalty slope: mean=-0.000016 +/- 0.000167, d=-0.10 (no delay dependence)
  - High-heterogeneity collapse rate: 3.86% vs Low-heterogeneity: 0.00%

  Conclusion: REJECTED — There is no period-dependent resonance window. The adaptive
  feedback controller uniformly degrades coexistence relative to both fixed and pulsed
  inflow, regardless of forcing period or delay. The controller's target-tracking behavior
  overrides the natural competitive oscillations that sustain coexistence in the cross-feeding
  system. When the controller detects low total biomass and increases nutrient inflow, it
  disproportionately benefits the faster-growing species, breaking the symmetric resource
  partitioning that maintains coexistence. This is a general warning that adaptive control
  in mutualistic ecosystems can disrupt the very dynamics it aims to stabilize.

FINDING #2: climate_delay_carbon_hysteresis_window (REJECTED, 216 runs, 11 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive carbon removal in a simple climate model with ice-albedo tipping
  point exhibits a reentrant delay window — intermediate sensing delays yield better
  climate outcomes than both zero and long delays.

  Method: A 1D energy-balance climate model with CO2 forcing, ice-albedo feedback, and a
  tipping point at +1.75C. Adaptive policy adjusts carbon removal rate based on delayed
  temperature observations. Four delay levels (0, 6, 18, 36 steps) tested. Composite score
  combines overshoot integral + 0.35 * removal cost. Each run executes ~12000 internal trials.

  Results:
  - Reentrant index: mean=-0.000610 +/- 0.004303, d=-0.14 (no reentrant structure)
  - Best gain (adaptive over fixed): mean=-0.048268, d=-281.65 (0/216 positive)
  - Per-delay adaptive gain (from 4320 sample trials):
    d=0:  mean=-0.402 +/- 0.464, d=-0.87 (25% positive)
    d=6:  mean=-0.529 +/- 0.747, d=-0.71 (25% positive)
    d=18: mean=-0.673 +/- 0.737, d=-0.91 (20% positive)
    d=36: mean=-0.890 +/- 0.754, d=-1.18 (10% positive)

  Conclusion: REJECTED — Adaptive carbon removal consistently underperforms the fixed
  baseline at every delay level, with performance degrading monotonically as delay increases.
  Near the ice-albedo tipping point, the adaptive controller's temperature-proportional
  response creates positive feedback: as temperature rises, removal increases, which reduces
  CO2 but not fast enough to prevent ice loss, while the removal cost accumulates. The fixed
  policy's constant low-rate removal avoids this cost penalty. This suggests that near
  climate tipping points, simple constant-effort mitigation may outperform sophisticated
  feedback-based strategies that incur escalating costs during overshoot episodes.

FINDING #3: wildfire_delay_adaptive_suppression (REJECTED, 218 runs, 16 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive fire suppression (PI controller targeting active cell count) reduces
  burned area with low sensing delay but becomes counterproductive under long delays.

  Method: 64x64 grid wildfire simulation with fuel, moisture, and wind. Adaptive mode uses
  delayed observations of active fire cells to modulate suppression effort via PI control.
  Fixed mode uses constant effort. Three delays (0, 4, 10 steps), two noise levels (0, 0.08),
  two wind strengths (0.20, 0.55). Each run completes ~300K+ trials. Key metric: adaptive
  minus fixed burned fraction.

  Results:
  - Mean delta delay=0: +0.002106 +/- 0.000011, d=185.80 (218/218 positive)
  - Mean delta delay=10: +0.002106 +/- 0.000008, d=253.23 (218/218 positive)
  - Delay inversion observed: 0/218 runs
  - All 12 conditions show adaptive burning MORE (100% consistency)

  Conclusion: REJECTED — Adaptive suppression is universally counterproductive regardless
  of delay, noise, or wind conditions. The PI controller concentrates suppression effort on
  current hotspots based on delayed fire counts, but fire dynamics are spatially heterogeneous.
  Suppressing one front redirects fire through less-defended corridors, a "whack-a-mole"
  effect. Fixed-effort suppression, distributed uniformly, avoids this spatial misallocation.
  The effect is tiny (+0.2% burned fraction) but extraordinarily consistent across >300K
  trials per run, demonstrating that even well-intentioned adaptive control can be harmful
  when the system's spatial complexity exceeds the controller's observation granularity.

FINDING #4: ising_delay_adaptive_quench (REJECTED — opposite direction, 204 runs, 9 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive quench control (adjusting cooling rate based on delayed interface
  density observations) reduces domain-wall defects at low delay but amplifies them at high
  delay, especially with quenched disorder.

  Method: 34x34 Ising model with Metropolis dynamics, quenched from T=3.6 to T=1.35 over
  260 sweeps. Adaptive mode uses delayed interface density readings to modulate cooling
  slope via PI control. Three delays (0, 4, 12), two noise levels (0, 0.04), two disorder
  strengths (sigma=0, 0.35). Each run completes ~14000+ trials. Key metric: adaptive minus
  fixed final interface density (negative = fewer defects = better).

  Results:
  - adaptive_better_low_delay: 204/204 runs
  - adaptive_worse_high_delay: 0/204 runs
  - Per-delay aggregate interface delta:
    d=0:  mean=-0.0074, d=-2.86 (all 204 runs negative)
    d=4:  mean=-0.0088, d=-3.88 (all 204 runs negative)
    d=12: mean=-0.0089, d=-2.38 (all 204 runs negative)
  - Disorder AMPLIFIES adaptive advantage (sigma=0.35 deltas ~2x larger than sigma=0)

  Conclusion: REJECTED — opposite to hypothesis. Adaptive quench control reduces domain-wall
  defects at ALL delays and disorder levels. Crucially, the effect is STRONGER at higher delay
  (d=12 > d=0), directly contradicting the predicted degradation. The likely mechanism: the
  delayed feedback creates an effective smoothing of the cooling trajectory, averaging over
  thermal fluctuations near the critical point. This slower, smoothed response prevents the
  system from quenching too rapidly through the critical region, allowing more complete
  domain coarsening. Disorder amplifies this benefit because disordered systems are more
  sensitive to quench rate variations. This is an example of "beneficial sluggishness" — where
  control delay improves outcomes by preventing over-reaction to transient fluctuations.

FINDING #5: metapop_corridor_delay_forecast (REJECTED, 489 runs, 20 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive corridor activation (forecast-driven switching between high and low
  dispersal) improves metapopulation persistence under climate velocity only when
  implementation delay and forecast error are both low.

  Method: 60-patch ring metapopulation with Allee effects tracking a shifting climate
  envelope. Adaptive mode uses forecast of future climate position to toggle corridor
  intensity. Fixed mode uses constant corridor rate. Three delays (2, 10, 20), three
  forecast noise levels (0, 0.02, 0.05). Key metric: adaptive minus fixed persistence
  fraction.

  Results:
  - adaptive_helps_low_delay: 0/489 runs
  - adaptive_hurts_high_delay: 489/489 runs
  - Per-delay aggregate persistence delta:
    d=2:  mean=-0.01909, d=-6.36 (0/489 positive)
    d=10: mean=-0.02051, d=-6.00 (0/489 positive)
    d=20: mean=-0.01979, d=-10.07 (0/489 positive)
  - Effect is delay-independent (~-2% at all delays)

  Conclusion: REJECTED — Adaptive corridor switching consistently reduces persistence by
  ~2% compared to fixed corridors, with no delay dependence. The on/off toggling of
  corridor intensity disrupts ongoing dispersal streams: during low-corridor phases, migrants
  in transit face reduced connectivity, creating population sinks. Fixed corridors maintain
  a steady dispersal highway that allows continuous range-tracking. In metapopulations with
  Allee effects, even brief connectivity interruptions can trigger local extinctions that
  cascade across the network. This finding has conservation implications: dynamic corridor
  management strategies that switch between habitat connectivity modes may be worse than
  maintaining constant moderate connectivity.

FINDING #6: sis_delay_adaptive_rewiring_tradeoff (NO EFFECT, 168 runs, 9 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Delayed adaptive rewiring in an SIS epidemic on a network creates a
  beneficial tradeoff where moderate delay outperforms both zero and high delay.

  Method: 240-node network with SIS dynamics. Adaptive rewiring breaks SI edges and
  rewires to susceptible nodes based on delayed prevalence observations. Three reactivity
  levels (eta=0.8, 1.4, 2.0), four delays (0, 2, 6, 12). Score combines prevalence
  reduction, fragmentation cost, and effort.

  Results:
  - Delay win fraction: mean=0.5010 +/- 0.0124 (indistinguishable from 0.50)
  - Best score mean: 0.000022 +/- 0.000127, d=0.17
  - Positive trial fraction: 0.5001 (coin flip)

  Conclusion: INCONCLUSIVE — No detectable effect. The prevalence reduction from rewiring
  is precisely offset by the fragmentation cost it creates, resulting in a net-zero score
  at all delay levels. The tradeoff between epidemic control (breaking SI edges reduces
  transmission) and network fragmentation (isolating susceptible nodes into disconnected
  components) is inherently self-balancing in this parameter regime: aggressive rewiring
  controls the epidemic but fragments the giant component, while conservative rewiring
  preserves connectivity but allows spread. The delay parameter has no leverage because the
  fundamental tradeoff is structural, not temporal.
