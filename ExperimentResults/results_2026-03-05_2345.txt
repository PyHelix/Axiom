FINDING #1: brusselator_delay_sensor_noise (REJECTED delay inversion, 33 seeds, 7 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive feed control improves spatial pattern quality at low
  control delay but degrades it at high delay under sensor noise and heterogeneity.

  Method: 2D Brusselator reaction-diffusion on a 72x72 grid. Adaptive mode adjusts
  the bifurcation parameter b based on delayed, noisy observations of spatial variance,
  trying to maintain target variance=0.028. Fixed mode holds b=2.95. Pattern quality
  scored as 0.65*var(u) + 0.35*gradient. Delays: 0, 6, 18 steps; sensor noise: 0, 0.02;
  heterogeneity: 0, 0.15.

  Results:
  - gain_d0 (adaptive - fixed): +0.00387 +/- 0.00016, d=24.1, 33/33 positive
  - gain_d6: +0.032 avg across conditions, d=13-91 per condition, 100% positive
  - gain_d18: +0.0305 +/- 0.00055, d=55.7, 33/33 positive
  - NO inversion observed (0/33 runs)
  - Adaptive benefit INCREASES with delay (d18 gain ~8x larger than d0 gain)
  - Heterogeneity amplifies gain at d=0 (h=0.15: +0.007 vs h=0: +0.0001)
  - Sensor noise has minimal effect on gain direction

  Conclusion: REJECTED — adaptive feedback control does NOT degrade at high delay
  in the Brusselator system. Instead, delayed feedback paradoxically IMPROVES pattern
  maintenance more than instantaneous feedback. The delayed controller avoids
  over-reacting to transient fluctuations, effectively acting as a low-pass filter
  on the control signal. This stabilizing effect of delay is opposite to the
  hypothesized destabilization and suggests that in oscillatory chemical systems near
  a Hopf bifurcation, sluggish control can outperform responsive control by avoiding
  resonance with the system's natural oscillation frequency.

FINDING #2: sandpile_delay_adaptive_dissipation (NO EFFECT, 10 seeds, 4 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive dissipation reduces extreme avalanches at low delay, but
  delayed/noisy control overshoots and increases extreme-tail risk at high delay,
  especially under correlated driving.

  Method: BTW sandpile model on 46x46 grid, 2400 grain drops with 320 burn-in.
  Adaptive strategy adjusts dissipation probability based on delayed avalanche
  magnitude signal. Fixed dissipation at p=0.028. Metric: probability of
  avalanche size >= 120. Delays: 0, 6, 18; sensor noise: 0, 0.12;
  drive correlation: 0, 0.70.

  Results:
  - tail120 adaptive - fixed = 0.0000 at ALL conditions (0/10 positive at any delay)
  - Mean gain exactly zero for d=0, d=6, d=18
  - No delay inversion (0/10)

  Conclusion: NO EFFECT — adaptive dissipation control has zero measurable impact
  on extreme avalanche probability. The sandpile's self-organized criticality makes
  the avalanche size distribution insensitive to small perturbations in the
  dissipation rate. The controller's adjustments are insufficient to shift the system
  away from criticality, confirming the robustness of SOC attractors against weak
  feedback control. N=10 is limited but the effect is exactly zero with no variance.

FINDING #3: seir_delay_fatigue_control (REJECTED delay inversion, 9 seeds, 3 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive contact control lowers disease burden at low reporting
  delay but increases cumulative attack rate under long delays with fatigue
  and observation noise.

  Method: Stochastic SEIR model (N=18,000) with 540-day simulation, seasonal
  forcing, waning immunity (210-day period), superspreading noise. Adaptive
  strategy implements tiered contact reduction based on delayed/noisy incidence
  observations, with compliance fatigue. Fixed strategy holds constant 20%
  contact reduction. Delays: 0, 7, 14 days; obs noise: 0, 0.15; fatigue: 0, 0.01;
  climate amplitude: 0, 0.20.

  Results:
  - delta_attack_d0: +0.234 +/- 0.001, d=257, 0/9 negative (adaptive WORSE)
  - delta_attack_d14: +0.239 +/- 0.001, d=348, 0/9 negative (adaptive WORSE)
  - delta_overload_d0: -8 to -23 days (adaptive BETTER for hospital overload)
  - No delay inversion (0/9)
  - Adaptive increases total attack rate by ~23% at ALL delays
  - But adaptive reduces hospital overload days by 7-31 days

  Conclusion: REJECTED — adaptive control does NOT reduce attack rate at any
  delay. However, the result reveals a public health trade-off: the adaptive
  strategy's tiered response flattens the epidemic curve (reducing peak hospital
  overload by 1-4 weeks) while paradoxically increasing total cumulative infections.
  Periodic relaxation of controls allows sustained transmission chains that would
  be suppressed under constant intervention. This illustrates why pandemic
  "circuit breaker" strategies can reduce peak strain while increasing total
  epidemic size. N=9 is preliminary; needs 30+ seeds for confirmation.

FINDING #4: rps_delay_jitter_adaptive_mobility (NO EFFECT — ceiling, 6 seeds, 2 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive mobility improves coexistence under phase-jittered forcing
  only for low-to-moderate sensing delays; long delay reverses the benefit.

  Method: Rock-Paper-Scissors lattice model (72x72) with cyclic environmental
  forcing and phase jitter. Adaptive mode adjusts mobility based on delayed
  predation pressure sensing. 1300 steps; coexistence = fraction before extinction.

  Results:
  - Coexistence fraction = 1.000 for ALL conditions (fixed and adaptive, all delays)
  - adaptive - fixed effect = 0.000 +/- 0.000

  Conclusion: NO EFFECT (ceiling) — all species survive the full simulation in
  every trial regardless of strategy. The grid size and simulation length are
  insufficient to observe extinction events. Experiment is uninformative about
  the delay hypothesis and would need parameter redesign.

FINDING #5: toggle_delay_adaptive_control (REJECTED, 218 seeds, 21 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive control reduces switch rate in the genetic toggle switch
  under low observation delay/noise but overcorrects and increases switching
  under high delay/noise.

  Method: Two-gene toggle switch (mutual Hill-function repression, alpha=8,
  n=2.6) with stochastic dynamics. Adaptive controller applies gain-scheduled
  forcing based on delayed error signal (x-y) with variance-adaptive gain.
  Fixed mode applies no control (u=0). Switch rate = transitions per unit time
  where x>y flips to y>x. Delays: 0, 4, 12 steps; obs noise: 0, 0.08;
  process noise: 0.02, 0.06.

  Results:
  - Adaptive INCREASES switch rate at ALL conditions:
    delay=0: +1.39 +/- 0.07, d=19.9, 100% positive
    delay=4: +1.45 +/- 0.09, d=16.9, 100% positive
    delay=12: +1.57 +/- 0.08, d=20.0, 100% positive
  - Balance fraction also increases: d=40-77 (adaptive keeps system near x=y)
  - Higher delay produces MORE switching (monotonic degradation)
  - Effect is universal across all noise conditions

  Conclusion: REJECTED (opposite direction) — the adaptive controller
  DESTABILIZES the toggle switch at all delays. The controller's error-correcting
  force (pushing toward x=y balance) fights the bistable dynamics that naturally
  maintain committed states. Rather than stabilizing one state, the controller
  keeps the system near the unstable saddle point between the two attractors,
  where noise easily triggers switches. Higher delay amplifies this by applying
  corrections after the system has already committed, causing oscillatory
  overshoot. This connects to the general principle that negative feedback
  applied to multistable systems can create oscillations — a well-known
  phenomenon in control theory (Hopf bifurcation via delayed feedback).
