FINDING #1: gene_drive_delay_resistance_window (NO EFFECT, 94 results, 3 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Intermediate feedback delay in adaptive gene-drive release creates a
  "sweet spot" that improves pest population suppression vs zero delay or long delay,
  by allowing resistance dynamics to partially unfold before adjusting release rate.

  Method: Simulates a gene-drive population model with wild-type, drive, and resistant
  alleles over 220 generations. Adaptive release adjusts drive-carrier release rate based
  on delayed suppression observations. Compares adaptive vs fixed release at delays
  d=0,2,6,12. Window index = mean(center delays) - mean(edge delays).

  Results:
    Window index: +0.000008 +/- 0.000023, Cohen's d = 0.35, sign consistency = 63.8%
    Suppression gain (adaptive - fixed) by delay:
      d=0:  +0.000582 +/- 0.000030
      d=2:  +0.000590 +/- 0.000007
      d=6:  +0.000682 +/- 0.000016
      d=12: +0.000674 +/- 0.000009

  Conclusion: NO MEANINGFUL EFFECT. Adaptive gene-drive release provides negligible
  suppression improvement (~0.06%) over fixed release, and this tiny gain is
  delay-independent. The gene-drive system's homing dynamics dominate outcome
  regardless of release-policy feedback delay. The resistance evolution rate is too
  slow relative to the drive-homing rate for delayed observations to matter. Only
  3 hosts contributed; further replication may be needed but effects are likely real
  given the mechanistic explanation.

FINDING #2: interdep_flow_memory_shedding_tradeoff (REJECTED, 238 results, 15 hosts)

  Hypothesis: In interdependent infrastructure networks under cascading failure,
  a load-shedding controller with intermediate memory window (3-8 steps) outperforms
  both reactive (1-step) and long-memory (16-step) controllers at preventing blackouts.

  Method: Simulates two coupled networks (384 nodes each) with lognormal loads,
  random interdependencies, and equal-load-sharing stress redistribution. Controller
  observes historical overload signal with variable memory window (w=1,3,8,16) and
  forecast bias (-0.15, 0, +0.15). Measures final network survival fraction.
  Medium window gain = mean(w=3,8 survival) - mean(w=1,16 survival).

  Results:
    Medium window gain: -0.001198 +/- 0.000974, Cohen's d = -1.23
    Sign consistency: 83.2% negative
    Best window mode: 1.0 (shortest memory wins)
    Mean survival across conditions: 96.2%

  Conclusion: REJECTED. Medium-length memory windows HARM cascade survival compared
  to reactive and long-memory controllers. The shortest memory window (w=1) consistently
  performs best. In cascading infrastructure failures, rapid reactive response to the
  most recent overload signal is more effective than smoothed historical estimates.
  Averaging over past overload history introduces harmful lag during fast-moving
  cascades, where conditions change faster than the memory window can track. This is
  consistent with real power-grid protection philosophy, where fast-acting relays
  outperform slow-averaging controllers during cascade events.

FINDING #3: bkfault_delay_adaptive_damping_window (INCONCLUSIVE, 57 results, 11 hosts)

  Hypothesis: In a Burridge-Knopoff spring-block fault model, adaptive damping with
  intermediate feedback delay (d=18 steps) reduces peak seismic event energy more
  than zero-delay or long-delay (d=54) damping, creating a reentrant window.

  Method: Simulates a 56-block BK chain with velocity-dependent friction, elastic
  coupling, and plate-driven loading. Adaptive strategy adjusts damping coefficient
  based on delayed event energy observations. Compares fixed vs adaptive damping
  across delays d=0,18,54 and fault heterogeneity levels sigma=0.10,0.16,0.24.

  Results:
    Mean peak reduction gain (adaptive-fixed): +6.8e-7 +/- 2.2e-6, d = 0.30
    Peak gain by delay: d0=+4.5e-6, d18=+2.1e-6, d54=-4.5e-6
    Mean extreme event count reduction: +4.7 +/- 6.5, d = 0.72
    Reentrant fraction: 33.1% of trials show mid-delay > edge-delays

  Conclusion: INCONCLUSIVE. Adaptive damping produces negligible reduction in peak
  event energy (effect ~10^-7, essentially noise). There is a weak reduction in the
  count of extreme events (d=0.72), but high variance. No reentrant window: d=0
  performs slightly better than d=18, and d=54 is worst. Insufficient data (57 results)
  and small effect sizes prevent a definitive conclusion. N=57 may be too few given
  the high variance in seismic event energies.

FINDING #4: moran_delay_migration_window (REJECTED — ANTI-REENTRANT, 91 results, 15 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive migration between two demes in a Moran process with fluctuating
  selection produces a reentrant window: intermediate feedback delay (d=3,9) prolongs
  time to allele fixation more than zero-delay or long-delay (d=18) migration.

  Method: Simulates two-deme Moran model (80 individuals each) with switching selection
  environments and diversity-responsive adaptive migration. Compares adaptive vs fixed
  migration rates at delays d=0,3,9,18. Window index = mean(center) - mean(edge).
  Measures time to absorption (fixation/extinction).

  Results:
    Window index: -34.59 +/- 18.16, Cohen's d = -1.90, sign consistency = 100%
    Absorption gain by delay (positive = adaptive extends time):
      d=0:  +13.02 +/- 19.62  (adaptive helps slightly)
      d=3:  -27.53 +/- 14.27  (adaptive HARMS — accelerates fixation)
      d=9:   -5.36 +/- 12.46  (adaptive slightly harms)
      d=18: +23.28 +/- 10.42  (adaptive helps substantially)

  Conclusion: REJECTED (ANTI-REENTRANT). The intermediate delays (d=3,9) produce
  the WORST outcomes — the opposite of a reentrant window. At d=3, adaptive migration
  accelerates allele fixation by ~28 generations on average, while at d=18 it extends
  persistence by ~23 generations. This anti-reentrant pattern arises because 3-step
  delayed diversity signals are just stale enough to trigger counterproductive migration
  bursts during rapid selection sweeps, but not stale enough to be harmlessly ignored.
  At long delay (d=18), the migration response decorrelates from selection dynamics
  and acts as random extra mixing, which happens to aid diversity maintenance. This
  demonstrates that feedback delay can create "worst-case" windows where the controller's
  response is maximally anti-correlated with the current system state.

FINDING #5: fiberbundle_delay_healing_entropy (NO EFFECT, 458 results, 21 hosts)

  Hypothesis: In equal-load-sharing fiber bundles with finite repair capacity,
  moderate healing delay can increase critical failure load at intermediate disorder,
  while long delays increase burst irregularity and reduce robustness.

  Method: Simulates 1400-fiber bundle with Weibull-distributed failure thresholds
  (shape k=1.5, 2.5, 4.0), equal load sharing, and delayed healing (d=0,4,16,64).
  Measures critical failure load, burst entropy, and burst rate.

  Results (failure load delta, delayed - immediate, at k=2.5 intermediate disorder):
    d=4 vs d=0:  +0.0000 +/- 0.0000, Cohen's d = 0.02
    d=16 vs d=0: -0.0000 +/- 0.0000, Cohen's d = -0.02
    d=64 vs d=0: -0.0000 +/- 0.0000, Cohen's d = -0.00
    All shapes d=64 vs d=0: Cohen's d = -0.07

  Conclusion: NO EFFECT. Healing delay has no measurable influence on the critical
  failure load of fiber bundles under equal load sharing. The critical failure load
  is determined almost entirely by the disorder distribution shape and the load-sharing
  rule, not by the timing of fiber repair. Under equal load sharing, the system's
  catastrophic transition is driven by the collective load redistribution at failure,
  which is independent of how quickly repaired fibers return. This null result
  contrasts with local-load-sharing models where healing delay might matter due to
  spatial stress concentration.

FINDING #6: schlogl_delay_adaptive_gain_resonance (REJECTED, 50 results, 10 hosts)

  Hypothesis: Adaptive gain control in the Schlogl bistable chemical reaction model
  exhibits resonance at intermediate feedback delay, where adaptive control outperforms
  fixed control.

  Method: Simulates Schlogl model with adaptive and fixed gain strategies across
  delays d=0,4,8,16,24,32 steps. Measures system output. Computes
  diff = adaptive - fixed at each delay.

  Results:
    Adaptive - fixed diff by delay (means across 50 results):
      d=0:  -0.0107    d=4:  -0.0079    d=8:  -0.0083
      d=16: -0.0182    d=24: -0.0230    d=32: -0.0216
    Resonance index mean: 0.068 +/- 0.105

  Conclusion: REJECTED. Adaptive control UNDERPERFORMS fixed control at every delay
  tested. The deficit worsens with increasing delay (from -1.1% at d=0 to -2.3% at
  d=24). The Schlogl model's bistable switching dynamics are better served by constant
  gain than by adaptive gain that reacts to delayed state observations, because delayed
  feedback in a bistable system can amplify rather than dampen stochastic switching events.
